## Feedback loop velocity

Every organization has a version of this question, whether or not
they've ever measured it: **how long does it actually take for
something you learn — from a customer, an incident, a market shift —
to become a real, shipped change?**

Most teams can tell you how fast any one stage of that journey runs.
Very few can tell you how fast the *whole thing* runs, end to end.
That gap — the invisible time lost in handoffs between stages nobody
owns together — is what this dimension measures.

### Where most organizations start

At the earliest stage, this cycle time isn't tracked at all. Nobody
can say, with any confidence, how long it actually took to go from
"we noticed something" to "we shipped a response" — because the
journey crosses too many disconnected teams and tools for anyone to
see the whole path. The first real step forward isn't optimization.
It's simply making the invisible visible: picking a start point and
an end point, and measuring what actually happened, even
after the fact.

### Where the real gains happen

Once an organization can *see* its own cycle time, the next
question is: what's actually slowing it down? Most teams can name
their handoffs. Far fewer can say, with current evidence rather than
guesswork, *which one* is dominating the delay right now — and fewer
still treat fixing that handoff with the same seriousness as
shipping a feature. That shift — from "we know it's slow" to
"we've funded and fixed the specific thing making it slow, and
proved the fix worked" — is where most of the real value in this
dimension gets unlocked.

### What the top of the curve actually looks like

At its most mature, an organization isn't just fixing one slow
handoff at a time — it's continuously compressing the *entire* loop.
A signal comes in, gets correctly routed to whoever has the
authority to act on it, moves through the system, ships as a real
response, and generates new evidence for the next cycle — all as one
connected motion, not a relay race between teams who never talk to
each other. The organizations operating here measure their response
time in days, not months, and that speed becomes a genuine
competitive advantage, not just an internal efficiency metric.

### Why this dimension sits differently from the other twelve

Every other dimension in this model asks: *is this specific part of
your delivery system good?* This one asks something else: **does
your system, as a whole, actually learn?** A team can score well on
every individual capability and still take months to turn a real
signal into a real change, if the connections between those
capabilities are slow or broken. This dimension exists because
speed of learning, not just quality of each part, is what determines
whether an organization can actually keep up with what it discovers
about its own market and its own product.
